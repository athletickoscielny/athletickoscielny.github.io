Why Koscielny left Arsenal

By James McNicholas Nov 15, 2019 69

Laurent Koscielny’s comeback ought to have been the feel-good Arsenal story of the year. Faced with a career-threatening injury, he fought back to reclaim the captain’s armband and lead his team out in a European final.

Sadly, the tale didn’t end as Arsenal might have hoped. Defeat in Baku was just the prelude: when Koscielny refused to travel on the club’s pre-season tour of America, it effectively made his place in the squad untenable. Within a month, he had departed for Ligue 1 and Bordeaux.

As Arsenal’s executive team circle their wagons rounds the embattled Unai Emery, they are keen to stress that the head coach has been working under difficult circumstances. Presumably, Koscielny’s departure figures prominently in their thoughts. It is quite remarkable that Granit Xhaka is not the first Arsenal captain to go rogue this year.

However, it seems the coach is not without blame in this situation. While a return to Koscielny’s native France was the driving force behind the move, the player had also grown frustrated with his situation at Arsenal. The Athletic understands that in that final season in England, Koscielny felt both overused and undervalued.

This is the story of how Koscielny came to leave Arsenal — from rupture, to rehab, to recriminations.

May 3, 2018. Koscielny is marking Atletico Madrid’s Diego Costa in the second leg of Arsenal’s Europa League semi-final. Less than 10 minutes into the game, Koscielny falls, thumping the ground in physical and emotional agony. There is no kick, no trip — just a simple shifting of weight, and an explosion of pain.

It is where this story begins, but where so much ended: Arsenal’s immediate chances of getting anything from the game faded instantly, and so did Arsene Wenger’s opportunity to finish his Arsenal reign on a high with the European trophy that had always evaded him. Koscielny’s night was over, his season too. What hurt most was the sinking realisation that the World Cup — his World Cup — was gone.

It was public knowledge that Koscielny had been carrying a long-term Achilles issue. Many outsiders assumed that he’d been something of a time-bomb, and that a rupture was an inevitability. However, the medical team at Arsenal were actually caught off-guard by the severity of the injury.

Achilles tendinopathy is relatively common in athletes, and does not tend to lead to a rupture like Koscielny’s. Studies have shown that only 4 per cent of chronic Achilles tendinitis patients will suffer a rupture — the idea that one is a precursor to the other is a fallacy. This injury was more down to misfortune than mismanagement.

Over the years, Wenger and his staff had found a way to adapt training schedules sufficiently to manage Koscielny’s problem, crucially without resorting to regular pain-killing injections. In the course of the 2017-18 season, Koscielny started 31 matches across Premier League and European competition. At this stage, he was relatively comfortable with his schedule.

Koscielny had nevertheless been considering leaving Arsenal in the summer of 2018. Wenger’s departure seemed to mark the end of a chapter, and Koscielny’s agent Stephane Courbis was aware of interest from both France and China. Koscielny knew his next contract was likely to be his last, and was keen to consider all options. However, the injury made his decision for him — for now, he was staying.

The club were very supportive of Koscielny during the early period of his rehabilitation, with chief executive Ivan Gazidis discussing plans for a contract extension and even a potential coaching role after retirement. Arsenal had already convinced Koscielny’s predecessor as captain, Per Mertesacker, to extend his stay at the club beyond his playing days. Gazidis wanted to keep the widely-respected Koscielny around too.

However, the immediate focus was his rehabilitation. Koscielny’s travelled to St Raphael on the Cote d’Azur to begin the long road back to football. Returning to France was a mixed blessing: while Koscielny relished a substantial spell on home soil, it also brought home the crushing reality of missing out on France’s World Cup campaign.

When France reached the final, Koscielny was a guest of the French Football Federation in Moscow. So near, yet so far: Koscielny didn’t feel part of the celebrations, and was disappointed not to receive a conciliatory phone-call from Didier Deschamps until September. As the nation celebrated, Koscielny mourned what might have been.

France’s triumph, Koscielny has since admitted, caused him more psychological pain than his injury. Speaking to Canal Plus, he said: “The World Cup will remain a dark spot of my career. The injury was hard to accept. The result was even harder because they won the World Cup. I have a selfish side. I tell myself that I could have been part of the adventure and won the Cup. And I would have it in a corner of my head for the rest my life. It’s difficult and no one can put themselves in my shoes.

“I was really happy for them, but also disgusted. You cannot feel like a world champion, as the 60 million French could. It’s a strange feeling that during the tournament I wanted them to qualify but at the same time, I wanted them to lose. That was my feeling at the time.”

This “dark spot” for Koscielny cast a shadow over his summer. It’s an experience he freely admits changed him forever: he has since referred to a “Koscielny before the injury, and a Koscielny after the injury”. It was a period of enforced introspection, and one in which his perspective shifted considerably.

Koscielny’s family life had always been of great importance to him. Speaking in 2016, he said: “When you are a father, you know your life has changed. Now you don’t live for you, you live for your kids. You want to give them a good education. It is difficult because when you play every three days, you go away and you don’t spend a lot of time at home. Sometimes, when you are tired, you don’t want to do a lot at home.

“Everything I do between now and the future will be for my children. I don’t stop living my life but I will live for my children.”

The injury forced Koscielny to confront the frailty of his footballing career and reassess his priorities. Suddenly, Arsenal’s gruelling schedule held less allure — especially without the reward of Champions League football.

Along with Wenger, a number of medical personnel with whom Koscielny was accustomed to working left in 2018. After Emery’s appointment, the Spaniard initially appeared to endorse Koscielny as his club captain. However, he hummed and hawed before eventually confirming the armband would remain with the Frenchman in mid-August. For Koscielny, isolated from the group by his injury, it was a trying experience.

A difficult summer was compounded by Gazidis’ departure for AC Milan, and contract talks with Arsenal were postponed. Koscielny’s renewed commitment to his family meant that China was off the table, but a return to France felt increasingly like an inevitability. First, Koscielny had to prove he could play again.

On December 13, 2018, the 33-year-old centre-back made his comeback as the Gunners beat Qarabag 1-0. It was an emotional night for Koscielny, but there was no time for reflection — just three days later, a selection crisis meant him being plunged straight into Premier League action at Southampton. The Frenchman looked understandably rusty in a 3-2 defeat, and was subjected to a barrage of unfair criticism.

After seven months out, he played four games in just 13 days. Emery needed results, and could not afford Koscielny the luxury of recovery time. For a player increasingly aware of his physical limitations, this was cause for alarm.

Koscielny continued to be deployed in a somewhat surprising manner. On New Year’s Day, he played 90 minutes in a 4-1 Premier League victory over Fulham. Just four days later, Emery named him in the starting XI for an FA Cup tie against League One Blackpool. Koscielny was none too impressed to be asked to travel to Bloomfield Road, especially when many other senior players had been spared the journey.

During the warm-up, Koscielny complained of a back problem and withdrew from the match. Members of the Arsenal coaching staff felt that Koscielny probably could have played but with concerns over the surface and in the midst of a difficult winter schedule, their sense was that this was an act of self-preservation. Given what he had been through, who can blame him? It’s certainly safe to infer that Koscielny’s back issue was nothing too severe: when Arsenal faced West Ham just a week later, he was fit to start.

Between April 11 and April 24, Koscielny started five games in just two weeks. As pressure mounted on Emery, so it did on Koscielny’s aching muscles. It was a period in which the player’s body and patience were both pushed to their limits.

Nevertheless, Koscielny kept on, displaying remarkable professionalism. In the second half of last season, he was frequently Arsenal’s best defender, inspired by the prospect of a glorious finale in Baku. Unfortunately, it didn’t come to pass. Koscielny’s career is one peppered by painful finals: the Euro 2016 one in which he narrowly lost, the Europa League final in which Arsenal were comfortably beaten, and the World Cup final in which he couldn’t play.

With the season behind him, Koscielny’s thoughts gravitated to France. With Gazidis gone, Arsenal’s contract offer had been reduced to one extra year on diminished terms. Without Champions League football, there was little incentive for Koscielny to accept.

Koscielny also felt that France offered a more lenient schedule for his creaking joints. In Ligue 1, he knew he’d be required to play once a week. At Arsenal, he would play up to three matches in seven days. It simply wasn’t sustainable. Koscielny has since admitted to L’Equipe: “I didn’t feel I could play 40-50 matches and I didn’t want to end my career with injuries. I’ve gone down a level, but to take more pleasure.”

Fundamentally, Koscielny felt that a move would prolong his career. It won’t have escaped his attention that Brazilian centre-half Hilton is a regular starter for Ligue 1 Montpellier at the age of 42.

There were several offers from the player’s homeland for Courbis to study — even Ligue 2 Lorient made an ambitious bid to bring Koscielny back to the club. Rennes were a strong contender, providing the biggest salary package. However, Koscielny was minded to join Bordeaux: they offered an exciting project with a new American owner, a longer contract, and proximity to Koscielny’s beloved Tulle.

Tulle — known as ‘The Town on the Seven Hills’ — is a two-hour drive from Bordeaux and the place of Koscielny’s birth. It’s a place dear to his heart. In 2014, he made a considerable investment to save the town’s historic accordion factory from closure. With family already living in the region, Bordeaux ticked every box. “Everything was in my thinking”, says Koscielny. “Football, my physical state, my wife, my children, the daily life we could have”.

It is now that Arsenal and Koscielny found themselves at odds. After nine years’ service, Koscielny and his agent seemed to be operating under the belief that he would be allowed to leave for a nominal sum. It’s perfectly possible that under Wenger and Gazidis, that might have been the case.

However, the new Arsenal executive team were not prepared to simply step aside and allow Koscielny to leave. They regarded him as an asset to the squad — under contract, and with another on the table. Having already lost experienced players like Aaron Ramsey, Danny Welbeck and Petr Cech without receiving a transfer fee, they did not feel it was in the club’s interests to let Koscielny go for nothing.

For his part, Emery was keen to keep Koscielny — especially as the club were pressing ahead with a deal for William Saliba, who would remain at Saint-Etienne for a year on loan. Koscielny was intended to bridge the gap; the player did not feel he could take the strain.

The battle of wills between player and club culminated in Koscielny refusing to fly out on the team’s tour of America. It made his position as an Arsenal player, let alone captain, untenable.

Koscielny has been reluctant to speak publicly on the matter but appears unrepentant. In an interview with L’Equipe, he said: “I can only say that I left with my head held high. All of those I worked with for nine years… everyone respected my decision to return home with my family”. Clearly, Koscielny did not feel he got the same degree of respect from the new management team.

Eventually a deal with Bordeaux was struck for €5 million. Koscielny might feel he was justified in thinking the club were applying a double standard — only a month later, Nacho Monreal was allowed to leave for just £225,000. It’s safe to say that deal raised eyebrows in the Koscielny camp.

It was a sorry episode, made worse by an unveiling video which saw Koscielny taking off the Arsenal shirt to reveal a Bordeaux kit. As Xhaka has since found out to his cost, you do not take off the Arsenal shirt lightly. It’s a sad end to nine years of excellent service. Koscielny’s perspective is that he did not set out to disrespect the club — merely to respect the wishes of his family.

The Xhaka comparison is an interesting one, as both men cited family as factors in the incidents that led to their fallout with the club — albeit in very different circumstances. In Xhaka’s case, he was incensed by his family being insulted and threatened. In Koscielny’s, he simply felt he had to put his home life first. What both stories share is a battle between familial loyalty and professional responsibility. Fans have been quick to censure both, but perhaps there is something to admire in their stance as husbands, as fathers.

It’s possible that time will redeem Koscielny. Certainly, he’s been the club’s finest centre-half of the Emirates Stadium era. He didn’t get the fairytale finish at Arsenal he’d have hoped for but his final chapter in Bordeaux will be consolation enough. For Koscielny, the story of his comeback ultimately became the story of going home.
